My Journey

From boarding school in India to biomedical sciences in the United States.

At the age of 12, I left home to attend boarding school at Good Shepherd International School in Ooty, India. Living away from my family from Grade 7 onward taught me independence, discipline, responsibility, and resilience much earlier than many of my peers.

Boarding school required me to manage academics, personal responsibilities, friendships, and challenges in a structured environment. It also exposed me to students from diverse cultural and international backgrounds, helping me develop adaptability, communication skills, and a global perspective.

Growing up around entrepreneurial influences, I initially imagined a future in business. Over time, however, my volunteer experiences, science education, research involvement, mentorship roles, and clinical exposure revealed a deeper calling: medicine. I discovered that healthcare brings together the qualities I value most science, service, leadership, problem-solving, and human connection.

Clinical Experience

Learning medicine through patient-centered observation.

Clinical Shadowing Intern - Kenya Coast General Hospital

Mombasa, Kenya • International Medical Aid • 100+ Clinical Hours

Through International Medical Aid, I participated in extensive clinical shadowing across surgical and radiology departments while observing healthcare delivery in a resource-limited environment. I saw how physicians and healthcare teams cared for diverse patient populations while navigating socioeconomic barriers, resource limitations, and complex patient needs.

  • Surgical and radiological exposure
  • Global health disparities and healthcare accessibility
  • Cultural competency and patient-provider communication
  • Multidisciplinary teamwork in challenging settings

Orthopedic & Hand Surgery Shadowing - Amanda Morrissey, PA-C

Physician Assistant to Dr. Robert Ablove • Hand & Upper Extremity Orthopedics • Oct. 3 & Oct. 10, 2025 • 16+ Hours

I shadowed Amanda Morrissey, PA-C, in a specialized orthopedic practice focused on hand and upper extremity care. I observed patient evaluations, treatment planning, and follow-up visits for carpal tunnel syndrome, hand and wrist fractures, tendon and ligament injuries, nerve compression disorders, arthritis-related conditions, and post-operative recovery.

A memorable encounter involved a patient who spoke a language unfamiliar to the clinical team. Through interpreter services and technology, the team gathered a medical history and communicated a care plan. This showed me how innovation, patience, and communication can reduce barriers to care.
